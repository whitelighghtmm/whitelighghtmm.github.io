Occupational Therapy



Does My Child Need Occupational Therapy?

Children with ASD can benefit tremendously from having occupational therapy (OT). At a fundamental level, OT helps children who have difficulty with focus, sensory integration, motor planning, and self-regulation. Many special needs children also have low muscle tone and lag behind on their gross motor and fine motor milestones, needing extra support to reach these goals.

A qualified OT is able to plan a therapeutic program to help children improve these areas through tasks that can involve music, movement, and sensory stimulation. Our partnering occupational therapist is able to carry out programs in our center, working collaboratively with our therapists for families wishing to have an integrated therapy experience.

Other areas that can be addressed through OT include working memory skills, visual perception, emotional management, and handwriting.

Occupational Therapist

We are privileged to work with Guo Hua, who is a registered occupational therapist under Singapore’s Allied Health Professions Council. After working with children with developmental delays through his clinical work at organizations such as Fei Yue Community Services and MINDSville. Guo Hua developed a keen interest in special needs. He has presented about his work at the Asia-Pacific Occupational Therapy Conference in 2011 and his research has been published in an international peer-reviewed journal in 2014.

Guo Hua has been trained in the following approaches:
• Sensory Integration Praxis Test (SIPT Certified)
• Reality therapy, Choice therapy – Basic Level (William Glasser Institute)
• Social Thinking (Michelle Garcia Winner)
• Interactive Metronome (Certified)
• Therapeutic Listening – Listening with the whole body (Vital sounds) (Certified)
• Cognitive Orientation for Daily Occupational Performance (CO-OP)

Guo Hua has a Diploma in Occupational Therapy from Nanyang Polytechnic and a Master’s in Science (Occupational Therapy) from Queen Margaret University. He values providing family-oriented therapy and parent education, as these are essential for successful intervention outcomes in the child’s development.
