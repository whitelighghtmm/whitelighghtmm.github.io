Speech Therapy



Many children with Autism have difficulty with using speech for communication. This may be due to language delays and/or articulation difficulties such as motor planning difficulties, oral motor issues and phonological disorders.

At ARN, Speech Therapy focuses on supporting your child’s acquisition of speech for communication. The approaches used include:

  • Oral Placement Therapy
    Oral Placement Therapy utilizes a combination of: (1) auditory stimulation, (2) visual stimulation and (3) tactile stimulation to the mouth to improve speech clarity. Talktools® are used to teach children where, when and how to place and move their jaws, lips and tongues to produce specific speech sounds accurately.

  • Oral Motor Therapy
    Children with speech delay may also have oral motor delay. They may lack awareness of their oral area or they may have weak oral muscles. They do not know where their jaw, lips and tongue are and how they should move them to make specific oral movements. Oral motor exercises, using Talktools®, are prescribed to increase oral awareness and strengthen oral muscles.

  • Communication through Routines, Activities and Play
    Children must be motivated to use speech for communication. It is meaningless if a child can say many words but does not use them for communication, such as asking for his needs/wants, asking for help/break/toilet, accepting (saying ‘yes’) and rejecting ‘no’). In Speech Therapy sessions, the child is encouraged to use his words throughout his day, i.e. in routines, class activities and play.

At ARN, Speech Therapy also supports your child’s feeding skills development.

  • Feeding Therapy
    Some children with Autism experience feeding issues. They may be fussy eaters due to sensory issues and/or the lack of motor skills for feeding. Speech therapy focuses on neutralising oral sensation and building feeding skills through pre-feeding and feeding programmes.

Will Speech Therapy Help My Child?

In addition to intensive 1-to-1 ABA-VB therapy, many children also benefit from having speech therapy. Communication and language acquisition often form some of the key challenges when it comes to ASD and developmental delays. A speech language pathologist can address some specific difficulties that our behavioral therapists cannot, such as oral-motor disorders, articulation, and fluency.

Before embarking on speech therapy, you should know that speech therapy is not a must for every child with ASD. Some children do not suffer from deficits in language and their difficulties may be more social in nature.

Another factor you need to ascertain is whether your child is ready to start speech therapy. In order to truly reap the benefits of speech therapy, your child needs to first acquire early learner skills such as being able to pay attention, listen, and cooperate. Based on your Individualized Education Program (IEP), our program supervisor can advise you when the best time to introduce speech therapy would be.

At the same time, it’s important to choose therapy approaches that are complementary to each other and not conflicting in philosophy. The speech therapists we have partnered with have a good understanding of ABA-VB from working alongside ARN’s therapists on a daily basis. They are comfortable with collaboration and communicate well with our therapists. In this way, you can ensure your therapy services are working cohesively and not in isolation.
